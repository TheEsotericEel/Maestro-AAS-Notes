# Fueling Motivation: Self-Determination Theory

> "Money stops you from quitting. It does not make you excel."

## 1. The Engine of Drive (SDT)
### What is it?
**Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)** posits that intrinsic motivation—the sustainable, high-octane fuel of excellence—requires three psychological nutrients.

### The ARC Formula
1.  **Autonomy** ("I choose"): The need to be the author of your own life.
    -   *Tech*: Choosing your IDE, your hours, or *how* to implement a feature.
2.  **Competence** ("I can"): The need to feel effective and capable of growth.
    -   *Tech*: Fixing a hard bug, learning Rust, getting a green build.
3.  **Relatedness** ("I belong"): The need to feel connected to others.
    -   *Tech*: Pair programming, helping a junior dev, open source contribution.

## 2. Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation
### The Extrinsic Trap (Carrots and Sticks)
-   **Extrinsic**: Doing X to get Y (Money, Grades).
-   **Intrinsic**: Doing X because X is satisfying.

### The Overjustification Effect
If you pay someone to do something they already love, they will start loving it *less*.
-   **Scenario**: A open-source hobbyist gets hired to work on their project 9-5.
-   **Result**: The "Play" becomes "Work." The Locus of Causality shifts from Internal ("I want to") to External ("They pay me to").

## 3. Engineering Application: The "Drive" Management
### How to Motivate Engineers (Daniel Pink)
1.  **Autonomy**: "Here is the goal. You figure out the path." (Don't micromanage).
2.  **Mastery (Competence)**: "This ticket is hard, but I think you're ready for it." (Flow state).
3.  **Purpose (Relatedness)**: "This feature helps doctors save lives." (Connection to impact).

## 4. Practical Protocol: The "20% Time"
Google and Atlassian famously allowed engineers 20% of time to work on *whatever they wanted* (Autonomy).
-   **Result**: Gmail, Google Maps, Jira Service Desk.
-   **Lesson**: Autonomy breeds Innovation. Constraints breed Compliance.

## ⚡ Key Takeaway
> **You cannot "motivate" someone.**
> You can only create an environment where their natural motivation is not strangled by micromanagement.
