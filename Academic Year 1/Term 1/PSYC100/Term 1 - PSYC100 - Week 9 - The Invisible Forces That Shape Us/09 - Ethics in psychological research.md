# Ethics in Psychological Research

> "Knowledge purchased at the cost of human dignity is not worth the price."

## 1. The Blood-Stained Origins
Modern ethical standards (The Nuremberg Code, The Belmont Report) were written in response to atrocities.
-   **Nazi Experiments**: Torture masquerading as science.
-   **Tuskegee Syphilis Study**: Withholding treatment from Black men to observe the disease.
-   **Milgram/Stanford**: Psychological trauma inflicted on participants.

## 2. The Core Principles (The Belmont Report)
### 1. Respect for Persons (Autonomy)
-   **Informed Consent**: Participants must know what they are signing up for.
-   **Voluntary Participation**: No coercion (e.g., "Do this or you fail the class").
-   **Right to Withdraw**: You can leave at any time, for any reason.

### 2. Beneficence (Do No Harm)
-   **Risk/Benefit Analysis**: The potential knowledge must outweigh the risk to the subject.
-   **Minimize Harm**: If you can test a drug on a simulation, do not test it on a human.

### 3. Justice
-   **Fairness**: Burdens and benefits must be shared. (e.g., Don't test risky drugs only on the poor).

## 3. Engineering Application: A/B Testing
We act as "Researchers" every day when we run A/B tests.
-   **The Facebook Complication (2014)**: FB manipulated feeds to be "Sadder" or "Happier" to see if emotion was contagious.
-   **The Violation**: Users did *not* knowingly consent to psychological manipulation.

### 📜 The Maestro Ethical Protocol
Before launching an experiment:
1.  **Consent**: Does the ToS clearly explain we experiment on features?
2.  **Harm**: Could this test cause psychological distress? (e.g., Showing graphic content to test moderation filters).
3.  **Opt-Out**: Can the user disable this "feature"?

## 4. The IRB (Institutional Review Board)
In academia, an IRB committee must approve all human research.
-   **In Tech**: We rarely have IRBs. **You** are the IRB.
-   **The Duty**: When a PM proposes a "Dark Pattern" test, you must be the dissenting voice.

## ⚡ Key Takeaway
> **Users are humans, not lab rats.**
> Just because you *can* measure it, doesn't mean you *should* manipulate it.
