# Wartime Research and the Invention of Modern Computing

## 1. Lesson Focus
This lesson traces how military needs during WWII and the Cold War drove the invention of modern computing. The pattern: urgent wartime problems → special government funding → breakthrough computing systems → follow-on technology that spreads to wider use.

---

## 2. The "Big Science" Pattern

The recurring chain from the 1940s–1960s:

```
Military problem → Special funding setup → Computing breakthrough → Follow-on tech/ideas
```

| Step | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| **1. Military problem** | Urgent need (firing tables, air defense, real-time tracking) |
| **2. Special funding** | Government absorbs financial risk via cost-plus contracts |
| **3. Computing breakthrough** | New machine or technique solves the problem |
| **4. Follow-on effects** | Proven technology spreads to other projects and commercial use |

---

## 3. Case Study: ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer)

### The Problem
- WWII artillery required **ballistic firing tables** — pre-calculated solutions for angle, wind, temperature, shell type
- Human "computers" (often women) + desk calculators were too slow
- War was moving faster than tables could be produced

### The Funding: Cost-Plus Contracts
- **Definition**: Government pays all costs plus guaranteed profit
- **Why it mattered**: Contractors could attempt risky, experimental projects without fear of bankruptcy if things went wrong
- **Financial risk**: Shifted to government, not contractor

### The Breakthrough (1945–46)
- **ENIAC**: First large-scale electronic general-purpose computer
- **Speed**: Thousands of calculations per second using vacuum tubes (not gears)
- **Programming**: Physical — plugging cables and setting switches (not software yet)
- **Result**: Proved electronic, general-purpose computation was viable

### Follow-On Effect
ENIAC's success opened doors for more government-funded computing projects.

## 4. Case Study: SAGE and Real-Time Computing

### The Cold War Problem
- 1950s fear: surprise Soviet bomber attack over the Arctic
- Needed to track many planes on radar and decide fast: *Is this an attack? Where do we send fighters/missiles?*
- Human plotting on radar screens was too slow and error-prone

### The Breakthrough: SAGE (Semi-Automatic Ground Environment)
- **What it did**: Network of radars + computers processing radar data and displaying it on screens for operators
- **Key innovation**: **Real-time computing** — the computer keeps up with the outside world as it happens
- **Difference from ENIAC**: ENIAC crunched batches of numbers and stopped; SAGE constantly updated in response to live events

### Core Memory (Magnetic Core Memory)

**How it worked:**
- Grid of tiny magnetic rings ("cores") with wires running through them
- Each ring magnetized one way (1) or opposite way (0)
- Currents through wires flip and read these bits

**Why it mattered for real-time systems:**
- Much faster than older drum or delay-line memory
- **Non-volatile**: stored data even when power was off
- SAGE's demands helped make core memory the standard

## 5. Comparing the Two Systems

| Feature | ENIAC | SAGE |
|--------|-------|------|
| **Era** | WWII | Cold War (1950s) |
| **Problem** | Calculate firing tables fast | Track Soviet bombers in real time |
| **Computing type** | Batch processing | Real-time, interactive |
| **Key innovation** | Electronic general-purpose computing | Real-time reaction to live data |
| **Memory** | Early forms | Core memory |

---

## 6. Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Firing tables** | Pre-calculated solutions telling artillery how to aim based on conditions |
| **Cost-plus contract** | Government pays all project costs plus guaranteed profit; removes financial risk from contractors |
| **Big Science** | Huge, long-term, government-funded R&D projects at universities and companies |
| **General-purpose computer** | Machine that can be programmed to do many different tasks, not just one specific calculation |
| **Real-time computing** | System that reacts to and processes data as events happen, not after the fact |
| **Core memory** | Data storage using tiny magnetic rings, fast and non-volatile |

---

## 7. Open-Note Review

**The Big Science Loop**
1. Military need creates urgency
2. Cost-plus contracts shift risk to government
3. Universities/companies attempt bold projects
4. Breakthroughs (ENIAC, SAGE) prove concepts
5. Success drives more funding and follow-on tech

**Why Cost-Plus Mattered**
- Made risky projects attractive to contractors
- Allowed for redesigns, delays, and experimentation
- Government absorbed cost overruns

**ENIAC vs. SAGE**
- ENIAC: batch calculations → proved electronic computing viable
- SAGE: real-time reactions → pushed interactive computing and core memory

---

## 8. Final Takeaway

Modern computing was born not from market demand, but from military urgency and a government funding model that absorbed financial risk. Cost-plus contracts enabled universities and companies to pursue experimental technologies like ENIAC. The Cold War's real-time defense needs then pushed computing from batch processing into interactive, live-response systems like SAGE. This pattern—military problem → government funding → breakthrough → spillover—defined the "Big Science" era and established the foundations of electronic, general-purpose, real-time computing.
