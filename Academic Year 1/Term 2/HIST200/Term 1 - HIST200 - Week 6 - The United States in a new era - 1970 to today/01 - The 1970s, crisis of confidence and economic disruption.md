# The 1970s: Crisis of Confidence and Economic Disruption

## 1. Lesson Focus
This lesson examines how the 1970s combined economic shocks (oil crises, stagflation) with political scandal (Watergate) to create a widespread crisis of confidence in American institutions — both the competence of economic management and the integrity of political leadership.

---

## 2. Economic Concepts

### Supply Shock
**Definition:** A big, sudden change in the cost or availability of key inputs that raises prices and slows production.

**1973 Oil Crisis Example:**
- OPEC embargo cuts oil supply to the U.S.
- Oil prices quadruple overnight
- Since oil is used for transportation, factories, heating → cost of making almost everything goes up
- **This is a supply problem** (production cost), not a demand problem (people buying too much)

### Stagflation
**Definition:** High inflation + high unemployment + weak growth — all at the same time.

**Why it broke old economic models:**
- **Phillips Curve** (older thinking): Expected a tradeoff — if unemployment is high, inflation should be low, and vice versa
- **Stagflation reality:** Both high inflation AND high unemployment together
- Made people question whether economic experts knew what they were doing

---

## 3. Political Crisis: Watergate

### What Happened
- 1972: Break-in at Democratic Party offices at Watergate complex, linked to Nixon's reelection campaign
- Real damage came from the **cover-up**, not just the original crime
- Investigative journalists (Washington Post) dug up secret money, FBI pressure, attempts to hide truth
- Televised hearings let Americans watch leaders being questioned live
- Nixon resigned in 1974

### Why the Cover-Up Mattered More Than the Crime
| Crime Alone | Cover-Up Pattern |
|-------------|------------------|
| One bad act | Suggests systemic dishonesty |
| Might be exception | Proves abuse of power is ongoing |
| Disappointment | Betrayal — "we were lied to for years" |

### Investigative Journalism
Reporters who don't just repeat official statements but **dig, verify, and expose hidden information**.

### Political Legitimacy
**Definition:** The sense that leaders and institutions have the right to rule and are basically honest and fair.

**What hurts legitimacy most:** A pattern of lying and cover-ups by top officials that only comes out years later.

---

## 4. The 1970s Crisis of Confidence

### The Narrative Arc

**Early 1970s mindset:** Post-WWII confidence that government experts + big institutions could manage the economy and keep growth going.

**Then the blows hit:**

| Year | Event | What It Did to Confidence |
|------|-------|---------------------------|
| 1973 | Oil shock (OPEC embargo) | Gas lines, shortages, everyday life more expensive. Question: "Can leaders protect prosperity?" |
| 1970s | Stagflation spreads | Old economic tools don't work. Question: "Do experts know what they're doing?" |
| 1972–74 | Watergate | Abuse of power + cover-up revealed. Question: "Who can we trust?" |

**By late 1970s:**
- Economic life feels unstable
- Political life feels tainted
- **Crisis of confidence:** Spreading feeling that the system itself might not be competent, honest, or controllable

### Carter's "Malaise" Speech (1979)
- Officially about a "crisis of confidence"
- Named the mood: people no longer believed leaders or that the future would automatically improve

---

## 5. Why Economic and Political Crises Reinforced Each Other

| Economic Problems | Political Problems | Combined Effect |
|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Maybe growth isn't guaranteed | Maybe leaders aren't honest | System seems broken on multiple fronts |
| Experts can't fix stagflation | Institutions cover up wrongdoing | Neither competence nor integrity can be trusted |
| Everyday life harder | Scandal on TV every night | Public mood shifts from concern to cynicism |

**Key insight:** Both threads were equally important. Economic shocks said "prosperity isn't guaranteed"; political scandals said "the people running this aren't trustworthy." Together, they deepened the crisis of confidence.

## 6. Open-Note Review

**Key Terms**
- **Supply shock:** Sudden change in input costs/availability that raises prices and slows production
- **Stagflation:** High inflation + high unemployment + weak growth simultaneously
- **Phillips Curve:** Old model expecting inflation/unemployment tradeoff (broken by stagflation)
- **Political legitimacy:** Public sense that leaders deserve trust and have right to rule
- **Crisis of confidence:** Broad feeling that institutions are incompetent, dishonest, or uncontrollable

**Key Dates**
- 1972–74: Watergate scandal and Nixon resignation
- 1973: OPEC oil embargo
- 1979: Carter's "crisis of confidence" speech

**Cause-and-Effect Chains**

*Economic:*
```
OPEC embargo → oil supply shock → stagflation → doubt in economic experts → lost confidence in government's economic management
```

*Political:*
```
Watergate break-in + cover-up → investigative journalism exposes truth → televised hearings → Nixon resignation → lost confidence in government integrity
```

**What Made Watergate So Damaging**
- Revealed secret actions (wiretaps, political sabotage, misuse of agencies)
- Showed top officials lying to public and Congress for years
- Unfolded on live TV — people could see it, not just hear about it

---

## 7. Final Takeaway

The 1970s crisis of confidence wasn't caused by any single event, but by the collision of economic and political failures. Supply shocks and stagflation made Americans doubt that steady prosperity was achievable or that economic experts knew how to manage it. Watergate and its cover-up made them doubt that political leaders were honest or that institutions could be trusted. Together, these threads transformed postwar optimism into widespread cynicism about whether the system itself was competent, fair, or controllable — setting the stage for ideological shifts in the decades that followed.
