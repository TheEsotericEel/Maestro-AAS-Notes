# Globalization, Supply Chains, and Digital Capitalism

## 1. Lesson Focus
This lesson examines how modern supply chains became globe-spanning networks of specialized suppliers coordinated by lead firms, enabled by digital tools. The core tension: this system achieves extreme efficiency through economies of scale and just-in-time inventory, but creates fragility when shocks disrupt critical suppliers.

---

## 2. The Modern Supply Chain

### The $700 Smartphone Example
| Component | Possible Source |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Screen | South Korea |
| Memory | Japan |
| Chips | Taiwan |
| Sensors | Europe |
| Assembly | China or Vietnam |
| Design/Software | United States |

**Result:** Parts from dozens of places arrive in the right store, right color, at the right time.

### Why Globe-Spanning?
**Main reason:** Cutting costs per unit by making each part where it's cheapest and most efficient.

---

## 3. Key Concepts

### Economies of Scale
**Definition:** When doing a task in huge volume makes the average cost per unit go down.

**Why costs fall at scale:**
- Fixed costs (machines, buildings, engineers) spread over more units
- Cheaper input prices through bulk buying
- Fine-tuned processes reduce waste and defects

**Example:** Factory making 10 million screens per month has lower cost per screen than one making 10,000.

### Deep Specialization
**Old model:** One factory makes the whole phone
**New model:** Separate factories for each component
- One factory only does screens
- One only does memory chips
- One only does final assembly

**Benefit:** Each repeats its narrow task millions of times and gets super efficient.

### Just-in-Time (JIT) Inventory
**Definition:** Instead of stockpiling big warehouses full of parts "just in case," parts arrive right before they're needed on the production line.

**Trade-off:**
| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|--------------|
| Less money tied up in inventory | More fragility if shipments are late/disrupted |
| Lower storage and financing costs | Production can stop if one key part is missing |

**Example:** In a strict JIT system, if a key supplier has problems (earthquake, strike, shipping delay), the factory quickly runs out of that part and the whole production line can halt.

---

## 4. Lead Firms and Suppliers

### The Structure
| Role | Function | Examples |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Lead firm** | Designs product, controls brand, coordinates network, sets quality standards, prices, timelines | Apple, Nike |
| **Suppliers** | Compete to win contracts, manufacture parts, follow lead firm's rules | Foxconn, various component makers |

**Key dynamic:** Lead firms don't make most physical products themselves — they coordinate networks of suppliers who do.

### Bargaining Power
**Why lead firms have more power:**
- Can move orders to different suppliers around the world
- Any single supplier is more replaceable
- Lead firm can say: "If you don't meet our price/terms, we'll move this order to someone else."

---

## 5. Digital Coordination Tools

### Evolution of Coordination
| Tool | Function | Impact |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Barcodes** | Cheaply track items as they move | What is this? Where is it? |
| **EDI (Electronic Data Interchange)** | Computers send orders/shipping info directly | Faster, more accurate communication |
| **ERP systems** | One big system showing inventory, orders, production | Integrated view inside company |
| **Internet platforms/portals/APIs** | Real-time data sharing across firms/countries | Global coordination at low cost |

### How Digital Tools Shifted Power
**Lead firm power ↑ because:**
- Easy to compare suppliers worldwide
- Easy to switch between suppliers
- Can run JIT more tightly (less buffer, more control)

**Supplier power ↓ because:**
- More interchangeable
- Must compete hard on price and terms

**Worker/state power ↓ because:**
- Production can be relocated more easily when labor or regulations get "too expensive"

---

## 6. The Efficiency vs. Fragility Trade-off

### Normal Times
```
Specialization + JIT + Digital coordination = Very cheap, very efficient
```

### Shock Times (war, natural disaster, shipping bottleneck)
```
One missing part + No backup inventory = Production stops
```

**Key insight:** No amount of software can magically create a missing physical part. The tight digital coordination that makes the system efficient becomes irrelevant when the physical supply is disrupted.

---

## 7. Open-Note Review

**Key Terms**
- **Economies of scale:** Cost per unit goes down with huge production volume
- **Just-in-time (JIT):** Parts arrive right before needed, minimal inventory
- **Lead firm:** Company that designs, brands, and coordinates the supply network
- **Specialization:** Different factories focus on single components
- **Digital coordination:** Barcodes, EDI, ERP, internet platforms enabling real-time management

**Key Trade-off**
| Efficiency | Fragility |
|------------|-----------|
| Low cost per unit | No buffer inventory |
| JIT inventory | Production halts if supplier fails |
| Global supplier network | Vulnerable to distant disruptions |

**Power Shifts**
> Digital coordination tools shifted power heavily toward lead firms. They could now instantly compare suppliers worldwide and switch between them, so suppliers had to compete on price and couldn't organize effectively. Workers and local governments lost leverage too because production could move to a new country with a few contract changes.

**The Causal Chain**
```
Globalization + Digital tools + JIT
  ↓
Lead firms coordinate specialized suppliers worldwide
  ↓
Extreme efficiency but extreme fragility
  ↓
In shocks: missing part + no inventory = production stops
```

---

## 8. Final Takeaway

Modern supply chains achieve extraordinary efficiency through a combination of economies of scale, deep specialization, just-in-time inventory, and digital coordination tools. Lead firms like Apple and Nike design products and coordinate globe-spanning networks of specialized suppliers, gaining bargaining power because digital tools make it easy to compare and switch between suppliers worldwide. However, the same features that create efficiency — minimal inventory buffers, tight coordination, and geographically dispersed suppliers — also create fragility. When shocks disrupt even one critical supplier, the lack of backup inventory means production can halt quickly, and software coordination cannot substitute for missing physical parts.
