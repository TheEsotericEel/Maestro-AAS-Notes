# Personal Computing

## 1. Lesson Focus
This lesson explains why personal computers spread rapidly in the late 1970s and 1980s. The key driver wasn't just cheaper hardware, but the emergence of complementary goods — software, games, printers, and training — that gave people practical reasons to buy PCs.

---

## 2. Complementary Goods

### Definition
**Complementary goods** = Extra things that make a main product much more valuable.

For PCs, complements included:
- Software (spreadsheets, word processors)
- Games (Oregon Trail, educational programs)
- Printers
- Training classes

### The Core Pattern
```
Because useful complements exist
  ↓
Therefore the PC solves real problems / provides entertainment
  ↓
Therefore people decide the expensive machine is worth buying
```

### Classic Example: VisiCalc and the Apple II
- **VisiCalc** = Early spreadsheet program
- Accountants and business owners wanted VisiCalc so badly they bought Apple IIs just to run it
- **Because VisiCalc existed → Apple II sales jumped**

### Home User Example: Games
- **Because Oregon Trail came out → more families bought Apple II computers**
- Games made PCs feel fun and educational, not just business tools

### Business Example: Bookkeeping Software
- Bookkeeping program saves time, reduces errors
- Makes PC suddenly solve a real problem
- Business feels: "Now this expensive machine is actually worth it"

---

## 3. Platforms and Lock-In

### What Is a Platform?
A "type" of computer environment that software and accessories are built for.

**Early PC platforms:**
- Apple II
- IBM PC (and IBM-compatible PCs)

**Key constraint:** Software written for Apple II wouldn't run on IBM PC, and vice versa. Same with many printers.

### Platform Lock-In

**The logic:**
```
Because complements (software, printers) work only on one platform
  ↓
Therefore users get locked in to that platform once they've invested
  ↓
Because switching would require: rebuying software, maybe new printer, relearning
  ↓
Therefore they stay where they are
```

### Switching Costs
**Definition:** The money, time, and effort required to move from one platform to another.

| Investment in Current Platform | Switching Cost |
|-------------------------------|----------------|
| Bought 10 games | Must rebuy for new platform |
| Own compatible printer | May need new printer |
| Staff trained on current software | Must retrain on new software |
| Learned how everything works | Must relearn |

**Example scenario:**
> An office keeps using its current type of PC because all its key software only runs on that platform. Even if a new, slightly faster platform appears, switching costs more than the benefit.

---

## 4. Why Software Mattered Most

For home users, software was the biggest driver:
- Games provided fun
- Word processors helped with homework and letters
- Educational programs justified the purchase to parents

Training helped, but often people learned because there was software they wanted to use.

---

## 5. Open-Note Review

**Key Terms**
- **Complementary goods:** Things around a product that make it more valuable
- **Platform:** Type of computer environment that software/accessories are built for
- **Platform lock-in:** Users stay with a platform because their investments tie them to it
- **Switching costs:** Money, time, and effort required to change platforms

**Key Examples**
| Complement | Platform | Effect |
|------------|----------|--------|
| VisiCalc (spreadsheet) | Apple II | Business users bought Apple IIs to run it |
| Oregon Trail (game) | Apple II | Families bought Apple IIs for entertainment/education |
| Bookkeeping software | Various | Small businesses justified PC purchase |

**The Core Logic of PC Spread**
```
Mid-1970s: PC = box of parts, mostly useless to average person
  ↓
Late 1970s–1980s: Complementary goods appear (software, games, printers, training)
  ↓
Therefore: PCs solve real problems and provide entertainment
  ↓
Result: Rapid adoption in offices and homes
```

**Platform Lock-In Example**
> Because a business has invested in software and training for one platform, switching to a new platform would cost more in retraining and software replacement than any speed improvement is worth.

---

## 6. Final Takeaway

Personal computers spread rapidly in the late 1970s and 1980s not primarily because hardware got cheaper, but because complementary goods — especially software like VisiCalc and games like Oregon Trail — gave people practical and entertaining reasons to buy them. This created a self-reinforcing cycle: more users attracted more software developers, which attracted more users. Platform lock-in then shaped which computers people stuck with, as switching costs (rebuying software, retraining) made it expensive to change ecosystems.
